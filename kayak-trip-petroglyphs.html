Echoes in the Stone

An expedition to the Susquehanna River Petroglyphs.

A Lost World on the Lower Susquehanna

The Lower Susquehanna River holds the highest concentration of Native American petroglyphs in the northeastern United States. These rock carvings, numbering over 1,000, are a direct window into the cosmology and science of the Algonquian-speaking Shenks Ferry people who inhabited this region between 800 and 1,200 years ago. Before hydroelectric dams transformed the landscape, this was a dynamic world of rapids and islands, a sacred space where a vibrant tradition of rock carving flourished.

Gazetteer of the River's Rock Art

An expedition requires understanding which sites can still be visited, which are lost, and which have been relocated.

The Safe Harbor Group (In-Situ)

The primary destination is the Safe Harbor Petroglyph group, just below the Safe Harbor Dam, with over 300 carvings on seven main rock islands.

  • Little Indian Rock: Upstream from Big Indian, this is the crown jewel with ~150 carvings, including the famous solar calendar composed of serpent symbols.

The Lost Sites

The construction of the Safe Harbor and Conowingo dams in the early 20th century submerged a priceless cultural record, including the abstract carvings of Walnut Island and the geometric designs of the Bald Friar Group in Maryland.

Salvaged Petroglyphs

Decoding the River

Water levels are artificially controlled by the Safe Harbor Dam and are the most critical factor for a safe trip. Levels can change by several feet in minutes. Diligent, pre-trip research is essential.

Monitor the official dam operator site SafeWaters.com and the USGS Gauge at Harrisburg (HARP1).

  • Ideal (< 4.0 ft):Optimal viewing. More rocks exposed, manageable currents.
  • Caution (4.0-5.0 ft):For experienced paddlers. Stronger currents, some carvings submerged.
  • Experts Only (5.0-7.0 ft):Strong, erratic currents. Most carvings submerged. Not for viewing.
  • Hazardous (> 7.0 ft):Dangerous, flood-stage conditions. DO NOT PADDLE.

Launch Sites & Logistics

The best viewing occurs in early morning or late evening light. The low sun angle makes the shallow carvings "pop." Avoid direct overhead light.

Primary Launch

Conestoga River Park (River Road, Conestoga, PA). Has ample parking. It's a calm 0.5-mile paddle down the Conestoga River to the Susquehanna, then another 0.5-mile paddle to the rocks.

Alternative Launch

Pequea Boat Launch (Route 324). A good option for motorboats or those wanting a longer trip. Requires a 2-mile upstream paddle to reach the petroglyphs.

Guided Tours

For a safer, more informative experience, consider a guided tour. Paul Nevin, Uncharted Lancaster, and Shank's Mare Outfitters are all reputable local guides.

Land-Based Approach

You don't have to get on the water to experience the area.

  • Direct Views: The Safe Harbor Trestle Bridge on the Enola Low Grade Rail Trail offers stunning, direct views down onto the petroglyph islands. Access requires a ~5-mile (one-way) hike or bike ride from the Turkey Hill Overlook Trailhead.
  • Panoramic Views: The Turkey Hill Overlook Trail provides spectacular vistas of the river gorge where the petroglyphs are located, but you cannot see the carvings themselves from here.

The Explorer's Ethos: Principles of Respectful Visitation

The petroglyphs have survived for a millennium. Their continued existence depends on your actions. Adherence to a strict preservation ethic is a requirement for anyone who visits these sacred and fragile sites.

Best Practices for Viewing

Gently splash water on the carvings to make them stand out. Use low-angle morning or evening light. A camera's LCD screen can also help reveal details.
